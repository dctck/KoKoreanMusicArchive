Three Korean Spirits

Understanding these emotional concepts is essential for authentic interpretation of Korean choral music. Click each to learn more.

흥 [hɯŋ, Heung]

The spirit of collective joy and ecstasy in Korean culture

▼
[Click to watch: Traditional Festival]
흥: 재미나 즐거움을 일어나게 하는 감정
An emotion or sensation which causes joy or fun

Ju Hyun Lee and Myung Hee Lee at Dongseo University defined Heung as a status of ecstasy with joy and fun. They note that Heung is contagious—a shared emotional experience.

This Heung underlies every type of Korean traditional performing arts such as folk songs, mask dance, and pan-so-ri. It's based more on the person experiencing the emotion rather than the object causing it.

Heung possibly emerged from Korea's long agricultural history, which developed a relation-based society. This spirit dominates other emotions in Korean people—even when sad, Koreans often dance and sing as a way to vent their feelings.

한 [hɑn, Han]

Deep sorrow, longing, and the dignified acceptance of loss

▼
[Click to watch: Sal-pu-ri Dance]
한: 몹시 원망스럽고 억울하거나 안타깝고 슬퍼 응어리진 마음
A heart with sadness, resentment, and/or chagrin

According to Professor Insik Kim of Chung Ang University, Han includes feelings of loss. It is a state after grieving—the loss of a lover, country, or deeply cherished ideal.

Han is a spirit of combined emotions: grieving and digesting all sorrow, oppression, longing, and missing someone. While this emotion existed throughout Korean history, it became widely shared during the 20th century through Japanese occupation, liberation, and the division of Korea.

This shared spirit of Han works as a spiritual bond among Korean people, creating deep emotional resonance in their music.

정 [tɕʌŋ, Jeong]

Deep affection, care, and the spirit of sharing with others

▼
[Click to watch: Jeong in Practice]
정: 사랑이나 친근감을 느끼는 마음
A heart of friendliness or love

Korean people share food, drink, gifts, joy, and even pain. An old Korean proverb says: "If you share the joy, the joy becomes twice. If you share the hurt, hurt becomes half."

This ongoing spirit of care for others is remarkably similar to the Biblical verse "thou shalt love thy neighbour as thyself" from Leviticus—though Korea did not widely accept Christianity until the early 20th century.

However, this Jeong is rapidly disappearing due to industrialization, urbanization, and growing individualism in modern Korean society.

Korean Vocal Ornamentations

These distinctive vocal techniques (sigimsae) define Korean traditional singing. Click each to hear examples and learn proper execution.

평음/평목 [Pyeong-eum/Pyeong-Mok]

Straight Tone - Purer than Western classical straight tone

▼
[Click to hear example]

In Korean traditional music, eum (voice, tone, pitch) and mok (meaning neck or throat) are interchangeable words. 평음 is a straight long tone.

This straight tone is straighter than the straight tone in Western vocal music—it has minimal vibrato and represents purity of sound in Korean aesthetic.

떠는음/떠는목 [Tteo-neun-eum/Tteo-neun-mok]

Vibrated/Shaken Tone - Accelerating oscillation from below pitch center

▼
[Click to hear example]

This technique produces tones with vibration or a shaken quality. The vibration range is usually from under the center of the pitch to the center of the pitch.

It typically starts slow and becomes faster and faster, creating an intensifying emotional effect in the phrase.

꺾는음/꺾는목 [Kkeok-neun-eum/Kkeok-neun-mok]

Bending Tone - Quick pitch bend expressing emotional inflection

▼
[Click to hear example]

Kkeokneun sori (꺾는 소리) is one of the most distinctive and expressive types of sigimsae in Korean traditional singing. It involves making a quick bend upward or downward from a main pitch and then returning, creating a subtle yet emotionally charged inflection.

This technique adds a dynamic, ornamental layer to the melody, allowing the singer to express a wide range of emotions—joy, longing, or sorrow—with just a single note. Unlike simple pitch bending in Western music, kkeokneun sori is closely tied to rhythm and phrasing, often placed at key melodic points to shape the flow of the entire line.

흐르는음 [Heu-reu-neun-eum]

Falling Tone - Smooth downward slide conveying release and closure

▼
[Click to hear example]

흐르는음 [heu-reu-neun-eum] is a vocal technique in which the singer smoothly slides from a higher note down to a lower one. This gentle downward motion creates a flowing and expressive sound, often filled with emotion.

It is commonly used to convey a sense of release, longing, or closure at the end of a musical phrase. In pansori, this technique plays an important role in softening the musical line and leaving a lingering emotional effect.

Academic research on Korean choral music, performance practice, and cultural context.

The Evolution of Korean Choral Music: From Traditional Roots to Contemporary Expression

Dr. Kim Sung-hee | Seoul National University | 2021

Examines the development of Korean choral music from its origins in traditional folk songs to its current state as a sophisticated art form. Analyzes how Korean composers have integrated indigenous musical elements with Western choral techniques.

Female Dominance in Korean Choral Composition: A Historical Analysis

Dr. Steve Ko | University of Texas at Austin | 2023

Examines why Korean choral music composition became a female-dominated field without a formal feminism movement. Traces historical, cultural, and social factors that led to this unique phenomenon.
